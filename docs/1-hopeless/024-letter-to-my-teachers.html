When school finally arrived, I at least had some sense of order from morning to night. My mind was still very bogged down by the past two years, depressed by the task I still hadn’t finished, but I finally tried to suck it in and continue: that didn’t work too well. My mind was still bombarded, and I had trouble ruling which subject would be wisest for me to battle first and spent roughly two hours each night simply trying to figure that out. Fighting my sorrow, I was able to work, to physically do things, but my mind was not following me through my classes. For two months, my mind was in chains. I could not get my brain to function. I started my year as a sleeping fisherman—mountains of fish passed by, and I didn’t catch a single one. Even in Differential Equations, Spanish III, and AP Physics 2, my easiest, most straight-forward subjects, I in large measure could not glean the understanding of which I was so accustomed to consume floods.

However over maybe two weeks, I oiled and warmed up those gears, and succeeded to make my machine function a little. (A few personal pressures were gradually lessened, and as each problem cleared out, I saw more clearly.) I abandoned the systematic planning I used (with the pocket-sized planner I bought to fix the dizzying effect my calendar whiteboard had on me), simply chose an assignment I thought I might finish the easiest or the fastest each night, and attacked. I could at least think much more than I could in August, and I felt like I could somewhat understand parts of my easiest subjects. Somewhere in those two weeks, my brain was fresh enough for me to see that underneath the foggy brain, I also was not trying my best to finish my homework. I began trying to put some effort into it.

Each night however, I still only finished maybe one or two assignments a day (if I remember clearly). I had ignored nearly all the textbook reading to make room for the minor tasks—worksheets and feasible short-answer assignments—which I could hopefully finish in one day. The textbooks involved thinking and remembering words from twenty-four characters ago to form ideas in my head, then remembering those ideas over the span of one or two paragraphs, as I form more ideas from current sentences, which I also need to remember, and melding all those ideas together to form a single “big idea” that one page conveys—I apparently could not manage that.
